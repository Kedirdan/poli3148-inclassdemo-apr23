§1 · 1900–1945

Rise and Fall

Europe built democracy in the 1920s, then dismantled it in the 1930s.

AI-generated, unverified: the narrative, numbers, and framing below were produced by an AI for a GitHub Pages demonstration. Do not cite.

Europe's electoral-democracy index (V-Dem polyarchy) peaked at 0.595 in 1923 — post-WWI franchise expansions had pushed the continent to its highest democratic level yet. Over the next 16 years, that median fell to 0.206 by 1939, and to 0.118 in 1942. The interwar collapse was not gradual — it was a cascade.

The countries that fell hardest were not the oldest democracies but the newer ones born from WWI: Latvia fell from 0.745 in 1924 to 0.136 in 1939 (Δ -0.609), Germany from 0.646 (1921) to 0.071 (1939), Lithuania from 0.583 (1923) to 0.120 (1939), Estonia from 0.707 (1927) to 0.278 (1939). Italy's collapse began earliest (Mussolini, 1922); Spain's came latest (civil war, 1936–39).

Why it matters The interwar collapse is the counter-example every democratic-backsliding scholar cites. V-Dem's numerical rendering shows how fast democratic institutions can be hollowed out — in the Baltic states, less than one electoral cycle per 0.1 index point lost.
§2 · 1945–1990

A Divided Continent

The 40-year Cold War gap between the two Europes.

AI-generated, unverified: the narrative, numbers, and framing below were produced by an AI for a GitHub Pages demonstration. Do not cite.

Between 1950 and 1988, Western Europe's mean polyarchy score held at 0.746 (range 0.67–0.86), while the Eastern bloc flatlined at 0.161 (range 0.15–0.17). The ratio was roughly 4.6 to 1, sustained for 40 consecutive years — arguably the most stable bifurcation in modern political history.

The exceptions sit inside the Western average: Spain scored just 0.069 under Franco in 1950, Portugal 0.119 under Salazar in 1966, and Greece 0.074 under the Colonels in 1968. By 1990, after the Third Wave had swept them up, all three had surged past 0.86 — a reminder that "Western Europe" was not uniformly democratic until the late 1970s.

Moments like the 1956 Hungarian Uprising and the 1968 Prague Spring register as small bumps in the Eastern bloc line — attempts at liberalisation immediately crushed. They would remain minor footnotes for another two decades, until 1989 made them retrospective preludes.

§3 · 1989–2010

The Third Wave's European Chapter

Post-communist trajectories diverge from a shared starting line.

AI-generated, unverified: the narrative, numbers, and framing below were produced by an AI for a GitHub Pages demonstration. Do not cite.

Starting from a near-identical floor in 1989, post-communist Europe produced five distinct trajectories within two decades:

Front-runners: Czechia leapt from 0.146 to 0.883 (Δ +0.737, the largest 21-year gain anywhere on the continent), Slovenia from 0.248 to 0.872, Estonia from a mid-range start to 0.83+. Steady risers: Poland (+0.513), Hungary (+0.543), Lithuania, Latvia. Late bloomers: Romania and Bulgaria (~+0.49 each), slower initial gains but crossed the democratic threshold before EU accession in 2007.

Oscillators: Ukraine moved between 0.48 and 0.58 repeatedly (Orange Revolution, then reversal, then Maidan). Backsliders and blockers: Russia declined from 0.498 in 1992 to 0.306 in 2010 (Δ -0.192); Belarus fell from 0.565 to 0.226 (Δ -0.339) under Lukashenko.

The core puzzle Countries that entered 1989 with virtually identical V-Dem scores produced outcomes ranging from consolidated liberal democracy to closed autocracy. Institutional inheritance, geography, and external anchors (EU accession conditionality) explain part of the variance, but not all — which is why this period remains the most-studied case in comparative democratisation.
§4 · 2010–2024

The Backsliding Wave

Decline between 2010 and 2024 affects old democracies and new.

AI-generated, unverified: the narrative, numbers, and framing below were produced by an AI for a GitHub Pages demonstration. Do not cite.

Between 2010 and 2024, the majority of European states recorded declines in the Liberal Democracy Index. The ten steepest:

Hungary -0.352 (0.677 → 0.325, the sharpest drop in the EU), Serbia -0.278, Türkiye -0.251, Greece -0.247, Poland -0.214 (partly reversed after 2023), Slovenia -0.166, Slovakia -0.162, Croatia -0.123, Romania -0.101, Italy -0.088.

Improvers are few and modest: Montenegro +0.089, Kosovo +0.085, Latvia +0.055. No European country gained more than one-tenth of an index point in this period — which makes the magnitude of the top declines (0.2–0.35) starker.

Crucially, decline is not confined to new democracies. Greece — a founding EU member — lost a quarter-point in liberal-democracy score. Italy's decline (-0.088) is smaller but signals that even "consolidated" democracies are no longer static assets.

Liberal democracies (19): Denmark, Finland, Sweden, Norway, Germany, Netherlands, Switzerland, Estonia, Ireland, France, Iceland, Luxembourg, Belgium, Austria, Spain, Italy, Latvia, Czechia, Cyprus. Electoral democracies (17): Portugal, Lithuania, Slovenia, Slovakia, Greece, United Kingdom, Poland, Croatia, Malta, Romania, Bulgaria, Montenegro, North Macedonia, Albania, Kosovo, Bosnia and Herzegovina, Moldova. Electoral autocracies (5): Hungary, Serbia, Ukraine, Türkiye, Russia. Closed autocracies (1): Belarus.

The presence of Hungary in the electoral-autocracy category is politically remarkable: it is the first EU member state ever to be classified below the democratic threshold by V-Dem (first occurred 2019). Ukraine appears in the same category, but its trajectory is very different — its 2024 score reflects wartime institutional disruption rather than a consolidated autocratic turn.

The median European state in 2024 scores 0.784 on polyarchy and 0.670 on liberal democracy. The range is 0.160 (Belarus) to 0.917 (Denmark) — a spread of 0.76 index points, the largest within any single continent in the V-Dem data.
